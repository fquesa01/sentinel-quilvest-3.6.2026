Here’s a detailed list of **common human-resources (HR) investigations** an employer might undertake — along with the key **federal laws** and **Florida-state laws** that may apply. This should help you (for example at your company) understand the risks, triggers, and legal framework when you need to launch an investigation.

---

## Common HR Investigations

Below are frequent categories of investigations in the workplace, with a short description of each.

| #  | Investigation Type                                                  | Description / Typical Trigger                                                                                                                                                                                                                       |
| -- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1  | Discrimination (Protected-class)                                    | Allegations that an employee (or applicant) was treated less favourably because of a protected characteristic (race, sex, religion, national origin, age, disability etc). ([Employers Council][1])                                                 |
| 2  | Harassment (including sexual harassment)                            | Unwelcome conduct based on a protected characteristic (or perhaps more general—for bullying etc) that creates a hostile or offensive work environment. ([EEOC][2])                                                                                  |
| 3  | Retaliation                                                         | Allegations that the employer took an adverse action (or threatened one) because the employee engaged in protected activity (complained about discrimination/harassment, participated in an investigation, exercised some legal right). ([EEOC][3]) |
| 4  | Wage/Hour / Overtime / Minimum Wage Violations                      | Complaints about non-payment of wages, overtime, mis-classification, tipped employees, etc. Investigations may trigger by employee complaints or government audits. ([Ayo and Iken][4])                                                             |
| 5  | Health & Safety / OSHA / Workplace Safety Issues                    | Incidents or complaints about unsafe working conditions, failure to follow safety regulations, or work-environment hazards. Investigation may be required by law or internal policy. ([Employers Council][1])                                       |
| 6  | Workplace Bullying / Hostile Work Environment (non-protected class) | Even when the target is not a protected class or characteristic, the behaviour (bullying, verbal abuse, violence) may trigger an investigation — especially if the employer’s policy prohibits it. ([Scott Law Team][5])                            |
| 7  | Theft / Fraud / Misconduct / Embezzlement                           | Allegations of financial misconduct, theft of assets, falsification of records, misuse of company property. These often require immediate investigation. ([Employers Council][1])                                                                   |
| 8  | Policy Violations / Code of Conduct Issues                          | Violations of internal policies such as conflict of interest, computer misuse, social media misuse, non-compliance with handbook rules, substance abuse etc. ([Scott Law Team][5])                                                                  |
| 9  | Whistleblower / Internal Reporting of Legal/Regulatory Violations   | Employees report possible violations of law/regulation (for example environmental, safety, medical-records, compliance) and the employer must investigate. ([Rutgers SMLR][6])                                                                      |
| 10 | Reasonable Accommodation / Disability / Leave of Absence Issues     | Complaints that an employee’s rights under disability laws or leave laws (e.g., the Family and Medical Leave Act) were violated. Investigation may be required when accommodation was requested or denied. ([L&E Global][7])                        |

## Applicable Federal Laws

Here are several major federal statutes that often come into play when HR investigations are required:

* Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (race, color, religion, sex, national origin) – prohibits discrimination, harassment, and retaliation. ([Department of Justice][8])
* Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA) – prohibits discrimination based on age (40+). ([L&E Global][7])
* Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA) – prohibits discrimination based on disability, requires reasonable accommodation. ([L&E Global][7])
* Equal Pay Act of 1963 (EPA) – prohibits unequal pay based on sex for equal work. ([L&E Global][7])
* Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) – requires eligible employees be allowed job-protected leave for certain family/medical reasons. Violations may trigger investigations. ([Ayo and Iken][4])
* Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (FLSA) – governs minimum wage, overtime, record-keeping; investigations may relate to wage/hour complaints. ([DOL][9])
* Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970 (OSHA) – requires safe work environment; complaints may lead to investigations. ([Employers Council][1])

Other federal obligations:

* Retaliation protections under many statutes (e.g., cannot retaliate for asserting rights) ([DOL][10])
* Privacy and record‐keeping obligations, especially in sensitive investigations.
* Federal whistleblower laws may apply depending on industry/sector.

## Applicable Florida Laws

When your employer is operating in Florida (or has a Florida workforce), state laws add additional layers. Some key ones:

* Florida Statute §760.10 — Florida’s employment discrimination statute: prohibits discrimination on basis of race, color, religion, sex, pregnancy, national origin, age, handicap, marital status. ([Online Sunshine][11])
* The statute of limitations: In Florida, workers generally have up to 365 days to file a discrimination/retaliation complaint with the Florida Commission on Human Relations (FCHR) after an alleged unlawful employment practice. ([Florida Commission on Human Relations][12])
* Florida minimum wage & wage matters: For example, Florida law prohibits retaliation against employees who complain about wages. ([Online Sunshine][13])
* Florida requires prompt investigations of harassment/discrimination/retaliation claims. ([wannoslaw.com][14])
* Florida law also governs internal investigations when involving public agencies or state employees (e.g., Florida Statute §112.31895) which “receive and investigate complaints from employees alleging retaliation by state agencies.” ([The Florida Senate][15])

## Putting it All Together — What HR Should Do

Given your role (and the fact your company maybe in Florida or has Florida employees), here are some practical take-aways:

* **Trigger quickly**: When any allegation arises in one of the categories above (especially discrimination/harassment/retaliation) you must assess whether a formal investigation is required. Several sources say that investigations should be prompt, thorough, and unbiased. ([Brick Business Law, P.A. -][16])
* **Use applicable policy + law**: Ensure the complaint is evaluated under both company policy *and* applicable laws (federal + state).
* **Document everything**: Who was interviewed, what evidence was considered, how findings were reached. This is critical if litigation or agency claim arises. ([Miller Johnson][17])
* **Be aware of legal obligations**: For example, discrimination/harassment/retaliation claims often trigger *mandatory* or very strongly recommended investigations; wage/hour or safety issues may trigger investigations even without a complaint.
* **Florida-specific**: If you employ in Florida, ensure you’re complying with the Florida statutes (for example §760.10) and using FCHR deadlines, etc.
* **Train your HR/investigators**: They should understand protected characteristics, retaliation definitions, state vs federal law, how to conduct interviews, maintain confidentiality, protect the rights of the accused and the accuser.
* **Follow up**: After the investigation, ensure corrective action (if required) and monitor to prevent recurrence.
* **Get legal counsel when necessary**: Some incidents (e.g., senior leadership misconduct, discrimination claims with high risk) warrant outside counsel.
